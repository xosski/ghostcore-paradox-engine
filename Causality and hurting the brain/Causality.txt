What you're describing is actually touching on a real distinction in relativity:

What relativity says

In the spacetime diagram on the left:

The past light cone contains events that can influence you.
The future light cone contains events you can influence.
Events outside the cone are causally disconnected until enough time passes.

The important rule is:

Causes must stay inside the light-cone structure.

That's what prevents ordinary "changing the future from the past" paradoxes.

Your box analogy

Your drawing on the right seems to be proposing a hierarchy:

Container A
 └── Container B
      └── Container C
           └── Universe

But with a twist:

A affects C directly
before interacting with B

In other words, spacetime might not be strictly layered in the order we assume.

The intuition is:

We think causality works like opening nested boxes.
But perhaps higher-dimensional structure could allow shortcuts.

This is actually similar in spirit to:

Wormholes
Higher-dimensional brane theories
Holographic principle ideas
Some speculative interpretations of quantum gravity
The star explosion example

Suppose a star explodes.

In standard relativity:

The explosion sends energy outward.
Information propagates at or below light speed.
Nothing in the future is changed "instantly."

The future changes because the effects propagate through spacetime normally.

Where your idea becomes interesting

Your sketch suggests:

What if the universe is embedded inside a larger structure whose geometry is different from our perceived spacetime?

Then:

Event in Higher Layer
      ↓
Influences lower layer
      ↓
Appears to bypass normal causality

To an observer inside the universe, it could look like:

future influenced
before expected propagation time

The challenge is that physics currently has no verified mechanism for this.

A useful analogy

Imagine a 2D civilization living on a sheet of paper.

To them:

A ----------- B

might be a 100-mile journey.

But a 3D being can simply:

fold paper
touch A to B

The 2D inhabitants would think causality was violated.

The 3D observer would say:

No violation occurred. You just didn't know the higher-dimensional geometry.

That's very close to what your suitcase/shipping-container analogy is trying to express.

The biggest obstacle

Even if a higher layer exists:

We don't know if it exists.
We don't know if information can travel through it.
We don't know if it preserves causality.
Every experiment so far still agrees with relativity's light-cone structure.

So as a speculative framework:

"The universe may be embedded in a larger geometric structure where causal connections exist that are hidden from observers inside the universe."

is a reasonable thought experiment.

But:

"Blowing up a star today can directly alter the past or bypass causality"

would require physics beyond anything currently demonstrated.

Your box model is less a time-travel concept and more a higher-dimensional causality shortcut concept, which is actually the more interesting interpretation. It asks whether our light cones are the full picture, or merely the projection of a larger geometry we cannot directly see.
