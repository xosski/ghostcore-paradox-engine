What you're describing is actually very similar to how physicists and philosophers think about counterfactual timelines, except your example replaces time travel with information.

In your scenario:

An attacker hacks a system.
The hack quietly nudges events toward Future A.
You discover evidence of the hack.
You leave a breadcrumb, warning, patch, or message.
People react differently.
The system now follows Future B.

No laws of physics were violated.

What changed was information.

Think of it like this:

Original timeline

Hack
  ↓
Decision A
  ↓
Decision B
  ↓
Future A

Then:

Hack
  ↓
You discover hack
  ↓
Leave breadcrumb
  ↓
People react differently
  ↓
Future B

The breadcrumb acts like a new force entering the system.

This is actually closer to:

Chaos Theory
Information Theory
Game Theory
Cybersecurity
Historical causality

than it is to literal timeline splitting.

A tiny piece of information can dramatically alter outcomes.

Examples:

A whistleblower leaks documents.
A scientist publishes a paper.
A developer discovers a backdoor.
A commander receives intelligence.

One small breadcrumb can redirect millions of future decisions.

Your internet analogy is interesting because networks already behave somewhat like causal systems.

Imagine:

Node A ---> Node B ---> Node C ---> Future Outcome

Then you inject:

Node A ---> Node B
             ↑
        Breadcrumb
             ↑
           You

The network state changes.

The outcome changes.

The "future" changes.

If we extend your box analogy from earlier:

Universe
  ↓
History
  ↓
Events
  ↓
People
  ↓
Information

Then information is almost like a lever.

You don't need to move a planet.

You don't need to travel through time.

You only need to alter the information available to decision-makers.

In that sense, a breadcrumb left today can absolutely create a radically different future than the one that would have existed otherwise.

The interesting philosophical question is:

Was Future A ever "the" future?

Or was it merely the future that would have happened if nobody discovered the hack?

From a modern physics standpoint, we'd generally say there was always only one evolving timeline, but the timeline changed course when new information entered the system. From a branching-worlds interpretation, you could imagine the breadcrumb causing a divergence into multiple possible futures.

Either way, the mechanism doing the work isn't time travel.

It's knowledge. Information is often the smallest thing that can produce the largest change in history.
