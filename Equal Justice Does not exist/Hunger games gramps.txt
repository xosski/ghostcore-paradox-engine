You’re pointing at a real philosophical and legal tension that justice systems have struggled with for centuries:

equal punishment on paper does not always produce equal punishment in reality.

A 25-year sentence is mathematically identical across defendants, but existentially it is not.

For:

a 19-year-old, it may consume youth and identity formation.
a 40-year-old, it may erase career, family stability, and middle age.
an 80-year-old, it can effectively become a life sentence regardless of the number.

So the paradox becomes:

Is justice supposed to punish the act equally, or the human impact equally?

Those are not the same thing.

There are actually several competing theories of justice colliding here.

1. Retributive Justice

This is the classic:

“The crime deserves X punishment.”

Under this framework:

the sentence is attached to the act,
not the age,
not the lifespan,
not the consequences to the offender.

So the system says:

armed robbery = 10–25 years.

Theoretically “fair” because everyone receives the same scale.

But as you observed:
the outcome is wildly unequal.

2. Utilitarian Justice

This framework asks:

“What punishment best protects society and reduces future harm?”

Under this model:

age matters a LOT,
recidivism rates matter,
rehabilitation probability matters.

Statistically:

younger offenders are more likely to reoffend,
elderly offenders are among the least likely violent recidivists.

So from a purely pragmatic perspective:
locking up an 80-year-old for 25 years may produce very little public safety benefit.

3. The “Life Percentage” Paradox

You’re also describing something similar to proportional lifespan punishment.

For example:

25 years to a 20-year-old might represent ~30% of remaining life expectancy.
25 years to an 80-year-old may represent 100%+ of remaining life expectancy.

So the sentence ceases to be equal in lived impact.

The law generally avoids explicitly calculating punishment this way because it opens extremely difficult territory:

Should:

younger people receive shorter sentences because time feels longer?
older people receive shorter sentences because they have less time left?
terminally ill offenders receive reduced punishment?
wealthy people receive larger fines because money impacts them differently?

Once punishment becomes individualized by impact, equality before law starts morphing into subjective calibration.

4. The System Already Quietly Acknowledges This

Interestingly, courts already indirectly recognize this paradox.

Examples:

juvenile sentencing standards
compassionate release
age considerations during sentencing
life expectancy discussions in elderly defendants
parole eligibility
diminished capacity arguments

So while the law pretends punishment is uniform, in practice it already bends around human context.

5. Another uncomfortable paradox:

The same sentence can be:

merciful to one person,
annihilating to another.

A disciplined person with family support may survive prison psychologically.

Another person may be destroyed in 3 years.

So “equal years” never meant “equal suffering.”

That has probably never existed in any justice system in history.

6. The deepest paradox underneath this

A justice system wants:

consistency,
predictability,
equality before the law.

But humans are not interchangeable units.

The moment you fully account for:

age,
trauma,
psychology,
intelligence,
poverty,
lifespan,
upbringing,
intent,
rehabilitation potential,

you move away from rigid equality and toward individualized justice.

But individualized justice can itself become unequal or biased.

That’s the balancing problem every legal system wrestles with:

fairness through sameness
vs.
fairness through context.

And neither solution is perfectly clean.
